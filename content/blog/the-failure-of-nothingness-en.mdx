## The question that followed me around

Through a big part of my late childhood and adolescence, I thought about the same question almost every day: where did the universe come from? I went through several hypotheses, and none of them seemed to hold properly. God created it? But then where did God come from? The universe always existed? But what does "always" mean if time is a thing inside the universe? It came from nothing? But how does something come from nothing? The question looked simple, but everything I tried to use to answer it already brought along almost everything I was trying to explain.

One day, in the shower, an image came to me and stayed stuck in me. It was not a proof, or a properly formed theory. It was more like a feeling of unavoidable existence. As if absolute nothingness had an internal problem: it could not keep being perfectly nothing.

## The problem with imagining a beginning

Usually we imagine the beginning of the universe as an event. Something happened, and then something started existing. But that already smuggles time into the explanation. An event needs time. Something "happening" needs some kind of rule allowing happenings. Even imagining a place where the universe appears already assumes a stage waiting for it. So maybe the question "what came before the universe?" already comes a little broken. It uses concepts that maybe only make sense after a universe exists. Time, space, causality, law, energy, all of this might be a product of the universe itself, not neutral tools we can use before it.

But there is another way into the question.

## Maybe the beginning is not a thing

What if the deepest beginning was not particles, fields, forces, or laws, but something closer to absolute absence? Not empty space, because empty space is already a physical thing. I mean a real zero state: no matter, no time, no structure, no rule, no background, no stage where anything could happen. The strange consequence is this: if there are no rules, there is also no rule preventing something from appearing.

This is not a particle popping into a pre-existing void. That image still assumes space, energy, physics. The intuition is more primitive. Without a law forcing nothingness to remain perfectly nothing, maybe tiny differences can arise. Not particles, not energy yet. Differences. Minimal distinctions. Small breaks in absolute absence. Most would disappear immediately. Some would cancel themselves. A few maybe would persist. And maybe persistence is one of the most important words in the universe.

## Laws as habits of things that survived

A difference that disappears leaves no reality behind. A difference that lasts becomes the first kind of something. When differences persist, they can interact. Most interactions go nowhere. Some become only chaos. But some combinations repeat. Some reinforce themselves. Some patterns last longer than others. From the outside, this would not be physics yet. From the inside, if something like an "inside" ever exists, those surviving regularities would look like laws.

This flips the usual picture a little. We normally imagine the laws of physics coming first, as if they were invisible commandments written into reality before anything exists. But maybe laws are not previous instructions. Maybe they are habits of stable patterns. Not something imposed from above, but something selected from below. The universe would not have begun with law. It would have begun with instability. Nothingness had no lock on itself. Without that lock, differences appeared. The unstable ones disappeared. The stable ones persisted. Persistent differences interacted. Repeated interactions became regularities. Regularities became laws. Laws allowed fields. Fields allowed particles. Particles allowed matter. Matter allowed stars, galaxies, planets, life, and eventually this strange kind of thought that asks where all of this came from.

## The Big Bang does not answer everything

This does not replace cosmology, quantum field theory, or the Big Bang. The Big Bang describes the early universe when there is already a universe to describe: expansion, radiation, temperature, fields. But it does not fully answer a deeper question: why are there laws capable of producing a universe? The hypothesis here is that maybe laws are not the foundation. Maybe stability is.

Anything incoherent disappears. Anything self-contradictory cannot continue. Anything unstable collapses or cancels itself. What remains is whatever can persist. And if a structure persists reliably enough, observers that arise inside it eventually call that reliability a law of nature.

## An analogy with evolution

There is an analogy with evolution, although at a much deeper level. In biology, organisms are not designed ahead of time and placed into the world. Variation appears. Most of it fails. Some variations survive. Over time, survival shapes complexity. Life is not built so much from a blueprint as it is filtered by reality.

Maybe reality itself works this way at a more fundamental level. Not biological selection, but ontological selection. Possible disturbances appear. The unstable ones erase themselves. The stable ones remain. The universe is maybe what stable possibility looks like from the inside. If this is right, the universe was not built like a machine. It was filtered. It is not necessarily the only possible reality, or the best one, or one designed with us in mind. Maybe it is simply one of the structures that did not cancel itself out.

## Why is there something instead of nothing?

Maybe the answer is: because nothing is not stable. For absolute nothingness to remain nothing, there would need to be some principle preserving it. But a principle is already something. A law saying "nothing may happen" is not nothing. It is a structure. That is the hidden problem in perfect nothingness: it cannot protect itself without ceasing to be nothing.

If nothing has no lock, then something does not need permission. Something only needs to happen once in a way that does not immediately erase itself. From there, stability does the rest. I do not know if this is true. Maybe the universe comes from a deeper mathematical structure. Maybe from quantum gravity. Maybe from a multiverse. Maybe existence is just a brute fact, with no explanation underneath. But this image is hard to abandon: maybe the universe began not as matter, not as space, not as a divine command, but as the failure of nothingness to remain perfectly nothing.

The first things maybe were not particles. Maybe they were differences. The first laws maybe were not rules imposed from above. Maybe they were patterns that survived. And everything after, fields, matter, stars, planets, life, and thought, may just be the long consequence of stable differences learning how to persist.
