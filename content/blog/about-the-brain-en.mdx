## How the brain works

I was thinking about how the brain works and how it differs from the LLMs we have. That was what inspired me to think more about the brain today.

New connections between neurons are more mobile, more flexible. Over time, they stabilize. When the brain runs into a new problem, it puts way more neurons into play than it would normally need at first. After it learns more about the problem, the brain prunes the unnecessary connections and keeps an optimized path for solving the problem when it shows up again.

You know when people say that "working memory" only holds 7 +/- 2 concepts at once? Now I understand what that means. Each concept is represented in the mind by a pattern of neuron activation. So a concept is not stored in one specific neuron; it is the activation pattern. When you have 3 concepts in working memory, that means you have 3 neural activation patterns active at that moment.

The real limit of working memory is rooted in the interference that one concept causes in another. Let me clarify: if two concepts use an activation pattern similar enough that they interfere with each other, they are not activated at the same time, which limits the number of concepts you can have in your working memory. So the real limit is how much your brain can hold without destructive interference. Maybe there is something like constructive interference, where the overlap helps more than it hurts, but I do not know that yet.

Based on this, I have a hypothesis that maybe one of the tricks for keeping more non-interfering concepts in memory is the effective use of a "perspective". Let me try to make that clearer: the same concept can be interpreted in different ways using different perspectives, and that changes the neural activation pattern of the same concept. If you know how to use the right perspective for each concept, maybe you can carry more things in your working memory at once. Example: a vector can be interpreted in several ways. Some people think of a vector as an arrow in a 1D, 2D, or even multidimensional space; others think of a vector as a sequence of numbers; others think of vectors as a kind of matrix; others think of a vector as a set of characteristics (features).

Another thing that changes your capacity to carry more concepts in working memory is experience. The more experience you have, the better the brain gets at cutting the connections that are not helping and optimizing a concept that previously needed several activation patterns at once. This is what they call chunking. A chess master can look at a whole board at once and understand the situation right away, while a novice would see several different disconnected pieces. Experience is the name we give to the process of optimizing the information carried by neural patterns. The brain becomes less dynamic with age, but, in compensation, it becomes more efficient. That is the trade-off.

Today I also thought about the difference between how an LLM processes information vs. our brain. An LLM has perfect attention at a very expensive computation cost, $O(n^2)$: every word is compared against every other one. The human brain, in compensation, does not even try to do that. It compresses on the fly and pays a much cheaper cost, one that barely grows with the size of the text. The trade-off here is detail retention. While an LLM remembers every word perfectly, the human brain discards the details and compresses them into streams of meaning. You do not remember every word of a paragraph, you remember what the paragraph meant. And even more precisely, you have a memory of the sequence of neural patterns that were activated; one serving as the cue for the next.

Memory is layered. The shortest one, what we call working memory, is what you remember in that exact moment of the activity. Think of doing a multiplication in your head: it is the memory that holds the numbers while you work the problem out. Medium-term memory would be the vivid memory you have of the day you are in. You remember that earlier you did a hard mental calculation, but you do not remember it in every detail; it is a broader memory, but with less detail. And finally there is long-term memory, which is the set of memories that guide your behavior and your personality. Every day, when you sleep, long-term memory updates: the brain takes the things that happened during the day and changes the long-term store proportionally to the importance of each one. That "proportionally" is the important part, because it is what explains why you do not remember the mundane moments — the kind of moment you would keep on one day but not on another.

And it is exactly in that nightly process that dreams come in. While the brain replays the day, reactivating the neural connections that fired while you were awake to decide what deserves to become long-term memory, some of that material turns into a dream. It is clear that dreams have something to do with this replay, but exactly how is still not clear in science. My hypothesis, and not only mine, is that dreams are an artifact of the emotional reinterpretation the brain makes of the compressed information from the day. It is still hard to explain exactly how a normal day turns into a 6-meter creature chasing me, but a bad day can activate neural charges associated with fear or a dangerous situation, and that gets reinterpreted in the brain during the night as something much more sinister than it was.

## Structure is emergent

All of this led me to think about how human beings learn, too. And what I learned about the brain affected how I think learning can be done more effectively too. A hard lesson I took from my years of experience trying to learn things is that learning is by nature kind of fuzzy. The brain allocates more resources than it needs (more neurons), the connection between the neurons has not been established yet, and you have not yet acquired the ability to compress a complex scenario into one single pattern of neural activation, so you feel like you are working at maximum capacity (because you are).

The structure of knowledge, how you classify each object inside that system, how you represent and assign meaning to the interaction between those objects and the system (taxonomy), does not have to be formed beforehand. The order, the organization that is clear in your head when you acquire mastery of the subject, comes only after you go through the process of walking through the fog, of passing through the noise. It is analogous to how diffusion models work when constructing an image. They start with a completely randomized, random grid of pixels, and they make several processing passes to transform noise into information.

I am very guilty of trying to find structure in knowledge before I have even seen basically anything from it. But that temptation to find structure is only a symptom of the fact that learning requires a lot of emotional regulation to deal with the discomfort of being lost. But, knowing how the mechanism works, it is easier to deal with that. I definitely changed after I realized this.

Focusing only on classifying things while you learn can stall you, because the real structure often is not something you can define formally without running into some contradiction or exception. To get a taste of what I am describing, try thinking about the object class "chair". You have a very clear idea in your mind of what counts as a chair, but the moment you try to create a formal definition for it, you run into several problems. Not every chair has four legs, chairs can be made of different materials, not every chair needs to be resting on the floor, or anywhere at all (think of space). All the characteristics you usually associate with a chair are not a necessity.

And if that is the case, what is left for you to define what a chair is? If you are more pragmatic like me, you might think you can define a chair by its utility, its purpose: a chair is something you can sit on. Fair, but then is a table a chair? I can technically sit on a table, I can sit on the floor, I can sit on an entire planet, and that does not make those things a chair. This, by the way, is exactly what people in ontology, within metaphysics, rack their brains over. Classifying things precisely is surprisingly hard even for something as concrete as a chair; with abstract concepts it is even worse.

And yet you navigate these concepts all the time. You may not be able to define exactly what a chair is, but you carry a precise representation of it in your head, and it works. That is the point: the structure, the classification you have of things, is not something you impose from the outside; it emerges from your intimacy with the concepts. It arises naturally, after you have spent enough time with the material. And I believe this holds for a lot more than chairs.

And do not get me wrong; I am not saying you should not try to find structure in the process - that is natural. I am saying you have to accept that your first classifications will be quite wrong, and you have to have the patience to adjust your mental framework every time a new piece of information goes against the framework you established. And once in a while you are caught by surprise when you discover something you had not considered in your framework. Sometimes a single question is enough to make you question everything you have catalogued in that area up to now.

And this is where I think the whole thing goes deeper than just learning. I believe knowledge is fractal by nature, and the best way I found to think about it is with the Mandelbrot set. Picture the fractal as the object you want to understand. You can zoom into it indefinitely, but with a catch: each zoom level has to be rendered, and rendering here is exactly the de-noising process I described above. You reach a new level and it starts out blurry, noisy; you spend some time processing it until the image becomes vivid. And then you can zoom again, and again, and go through the same process at every level. There is no bottom. You can never de-noise the whole object, because that is not feasible; it is not in the nature of the object to have an end. In other words, you will never have complete knowledge of anything. But if you are pragmatic, that almost never matters: what matters is reaching a resolution good enough for what you need, and locking it there.

And that "locking the zoom at some level" is what we call abstraction. When you learn anything, you fix a resolution, the smallest level of detail you decided matters for the subject, and you work from there. Thinking axiomatically is just that: you start with the premises (the shallowest zoom level you set for the problem) and use them to build the full picture. Logic is the tool that takes you from one zoom level to the next, from $n$ to $n+1$. And philosophy, the way I see it, is what happens when you refuse to lock the zoom and keep descending into the fractal indefinitely, knowing you will never reach the bottom.

If that is true, it explains something that always bugged me: why the most basic things are the hardest to define. Go back to the chair. The reason the definition never closes is that the level of detail has no end; it is a fractal! You can zoom forever into the question "what is a chair" and you will always find one more exception, one more layer of noise to process. The structure emerges, but it never finishes emerging. You just stop at a point that is good enough.

## A method for digesting information

Recently I found a pretty interesting method for digesting information. Although some details are very specific to how I like to work and learn, I am sure there is something good you can take from it.

One thing I have been experimenting with is creating pre-consolidation notes. Let me see if I can explain this properly: you know when people talk about making a summary of what you are learning? So, the big problem is that you are trying to do exactly what I said earlier is not viable: you are trying to create structure too early. It will take you forever to get the structure and flow of ideas right and make everything organized. This kind of note is what I would call a post-consolidation note; a kind of note that only makes sense after you have already gone through the process of recognizing the content and are now trying to synthesize what you internalized. But that kind of note has to come after the pre-consolidation notes.

So what are these pre-consolidation notes I am talking about? They are notes that include questions you had during a study session; answers you found for those questions; answers you still do not understand; exercises you completed successfully; exercises you did not complete successfully; pages you read; videos you watched. It is a complete log of your progress in that study session. And I personally like creating one document per session.

The important point here is that you are honest about what you did and what you know. Instead of trying to copy a definition or explanation you read, try to capture what your brain actually understood from it. This is not the same advice as "write in your own words", because that command is very restrictive; it implicitly asks you not to use the same words, or any kind of rephrasing of the information. Not always, but once in a while, the definition you read, or the explanation you saw, was captured with maximum effectiveness and you actually understood and internalized that information in exactly the way it was received. In that case, if you can reproduce it because it is internalized and clear in your mind, I think it is harmless to simply use the same words.

One of the most interesting and effective tools for learning anything is questions. Questions are to knowledge what a chisel is to a marble sculpture. They help you in the process of de-noising information. You begin a subject with a very vague idea, and the questions help you assign meaning to that concept, the process of encoding information in the brain. This is one of the reasons exercises are so effective. Exercises are probes of your mental model. Plus, they help with the permanence of information. Every time you are forced to pull information out of your brain (recall), it becomes a stronger signal when the brain makes the transition from memories in RAM to long-term memory.

This is the document where you should feel free to be dumb too. The idea is not to share its content; it is to help you understand where you are, help you concentrate on the next step, and later help you synthesize the information you have already seen. The cool thing about documenting your learning process this way is that you can look back and see where you got stuck, why, and what unstuck you.

About the specific way I implement this currently, I create a directory in my Obsidian dedicated to a subject, and I create one file per study session. Usually a study session goes from 30 min to 90 min, or more if you are in the flow, or less if you are distracted. In it, I put metadata like day, date, time, duration, time frame (from what time to what time), tags, subject name, etc. In the document itself, everything is kind of free-style. There are no predefined sections. The structure emerges from your notes. I do not worry too much about formatting, headers, or any of that. That can be done later when the study session ends. The important thing is to write down what you are doing and solving most of the time. I like writing down my line of reasoning. It helps me understand where I am stuck and makes it clearer how to move forward.

## What are `X_train`, `y_train`, `X_test`?

`X_train` is the matrix of cars and their features. Each entry is a car, each column is a feature of that car.

`y_train` is the classification values of each car where position i refers to car i, and the value in that position is binary, 0 or 1, where 0 is efficient and 1 inefficient.

`X_test` is the matrix of cars we are going to test the algorithm on. They are cars outside the training set that the algorithm will use inference to predict their efficiency. This matrix has the same number of columns as `X_train` because it contains the same number of features, of course.

## So in this function I need to:

1. For each entry, car, in `X_test`, calculate the distance between that car and the cars in `X_train`, in order of distance.
2. Get the `k` cars closest to car `i` in `X_test`.
3. Classify car `i` based on the majority class from the list of the `k` cars.

Does `X_train` have a classification column? No, classification is in `y_train`. So I do not need to worry about filtering out the last column.

## Metrics

Apparently there are different terms assigned to different metrics for how correct a prediction was. Below are some of those concepts, how you compute them, and how you interpret them:

* **accuracy:** number of correct guesses over the total number of comparisons between labels and predictions.
* **precision:** If the classifier says the car is efficient, what is the probability that the classifier is correct?

$$
\frac{TP}{TP + FP}
$$

* **recall:** Among all efficient cars, how many was the classifier able to capture?

$$
\frac{TP} {TP + FN}
$$

* **F1**

$$
\frac{2 × \mathrm{precision} × \mathrm{recall}}{\mathrm{precision} + \mathrm{recall}}
$$
